Welcome to Puppy Parenthood

You're not just training a dog—you're raising a companion and building a lifelong connection. Here's everything you need to know to start off on the right paw.

Before Your Puppy Arrives

Research your breed. Understanding your dog's natural tendencies will help you work with them, not against them. Every breed has different energy levels, social needs, and quirks.

Puppy-proof your space. Remove cords, toxic plants, and small objects. Use baby gates to create a contained area—and make it smaller than you think. A limited space prevents accidents and makes potty training much easier.

Schedule that first vet visit. Book an appointment within the first week of bringing your puppy home. This establishes baseline health and gets you started with vaccines and preventive care.

Remember the mindset. You're not training your dog—you're raising them. Focus on building connection and trust, and everything else will follow.

The First Day Home

Bring something familiar. If possible, take a towel and rub it on the puppy's parents or littermates. The familiar scent can help reduce separation anxiety during those first few nights.

Be prepared for car sickness. Puppies are often prone to motion sickness. Keep towels handy and don't panic if there's a mess—it's completely normal.

Take it slow. Your puppy is experiencing a huge change. Let them explore at their own pace and don't overwhelm them with too many new people or experiences right away.

Feeding Your Puppy

Start with what they know. Ask the breeder about feeding times and the current food brand. Keep the routine consistent at first, then gradually transition if your vet recommends something different.

Know what's off-limits. These foods are toxic to dogs and should never be given: chocolate, grapes and raisins, onions, garlic, chives, macadamia nuts, alcohol, caffeine, avocado, xylitol (artificial sweetener), cooked bones, and fatty table scraps.

Keep it simple. Stick to quality puppy food and healthy treats. Your dog doesn't need fancy meals—consistency and proper nutrition matter most.

Health & Housebreaking

The potty training routine. Take your puppy outside every 30 minutes to an hour, always using the same door and the same outdoor spot. Use a verbal cue like "go potty" when they're in position, then praise enthusiastically and offer a treat the moment they finish.

Never leave them unsupervised. During training, watch constantly or confine your puppy. If you'll be gone longer than they can hold it, set up a small pen around their crate with a designated toilet mat just outside.

Provide appropriate toys. Puppies explore the world with their mouths—it's completely normal. Give them plenty of safe toys to chew on so they learn what's theirs.

Don't punish accidents. Dogs won't understand what they did wrong. Simply clean up and stay consistent with the routine.

Socialization & Training

Start early with positive experiences. Introduce your puppy to different people, animals, sounds, and environments early on. This helps curb fear and aggression later in life.

Use positive reinforcement. Mark the exact moment your puppy does something right with a clicker or enthusiastic "yes!", then immediately reward with a treat. This clear communication helps them learn quickly.

Keep sessions short and fun. Five to ten minutes is plenty for a puppy's attention span. End on a positive note and they'll be eager for the next session.

Be consistent. Everyone in the family should use the same cues and rewards. Mixed signals confuse puppies and slow down learning.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Giving too much freedom too soon

A large space can be overwhelming and makes accidents more likely. Start small and gradually expand their territory as they prove they can handle it.

Inconsistent rules

If your puppy is allowed on the couch sometimes but not others, they'll be confused. Decide on house rules as a family and stick to them from day one.

Skipping socialization

The critical socialization window closes around 16 weeks. Early positive experiences shape your dog's entire temperament. Don't wait—start gently introducing them to the world right away.

Treating them like a small human

Dogs have different needs, communication styles, and ways of understanding the world. Learn to speak their language rather than expecting them to speak yours.

Forgetting it's a marathon, not a sprint

Puppy raising takes time and patience. Some days will be harder than others. That's completely normal. Focus on small wins and celebrate progress.
